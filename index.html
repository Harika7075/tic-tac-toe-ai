01 — The Algorithm

Minimax: planning against an opponent who's also planning

The engine doesn't just look for its best move — it looks for its best move assuming you also play your best move in response, every time. That's the "mini" and the "max": it maximizes its own outcome while assuming you'll minimize it.

01 — Simulate everything

For every empty cell, the engine imagines placing its mark there, then imagines your best response, then its best response to that — all the way to the end of the game.

02 — Score the ending

Each simulated game ends in an AI win, a human win, or a draw. Wins are scored +10 − depth, losses depth − 10 — so faster wins score higher, and if a loss is unavoidable, the engine delays it as long as possible.

03 — Roll the score back up

At each of your simulated turns, the engine assumes you'll pick your best option — the one worst for it. At each of its own turns, it picks the best option for itself. This alternation, repeated recursively, is the whole algorithm.

04 — Play the highest-scoring move

Once every branch has been scored, the engine simply plays whichever first move led to the highest guaranteed outcome.

02 — The Optimization

Alpha-Beta pruning: skipping work that can't matter

Simulating every possible game gets expensive fast. Alpha-beta pruning cuts off branches the moment it's provable that a rational opponent would never let the game reach them — without ever changing the final decision.

04 — Where This Goes Next

This approach doesn't stop at Tic-Tac-Toe

Tic-Tac-Toe is small enough to solve completely — the engine can search every possible game to its end. Bigger games need a different strategy.

Extension

Reinforcement Learning

Instead of searching the tree explicitly, a Q-learning agent could learn move values through thousands of self-played games — trading guaranteed correctness for the ability to learn games too large to fully search.

Extension

Connect Four / Chess

Both have far larger state spaces than Tic-Tac-Toe, so full search isn't feasible. The fix: search a limited number of moves ahead, then use a heuristic evaluation function — material count and board control, for example — to estimate positions the engine can't fully resolve.
